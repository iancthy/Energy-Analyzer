Energy Basics

What is Energy?

Energy is the ability to do work or cause change. Everything happening around us ‚Äî motion, heat, sound, electricity, growth, and even thinking ‚Äî requires energy.

Why Energy Matters?

  • Your body uses chemical energy to move.
  • Plants use light energy from the Sun.
  • Machines use electrical or mechanical energy.
  • Cars use chemical energy in fuel.
  • Phones use battery energy.

Main Categories of Energy:

Although there are many forms (thermal, chemical, electrical, nuclear), physics focuses heavily on mechanical energy, which includes:

  • Kinetic Energy (KE) - Energy due to motion. Faster or heavier objects have more kinetic energy.
  • Potential Energy (PE) - Stored energy due to position or condition. Higher objects store more potential energy.

Units of Energy: A The SI unit is the joule (J).

Work

What is Work?

Work is done when a force causes an object to move in the direction of the force. If no movement happens, no work is done.

Formula: W = F √ó d

  • F ‚Üí force (N)
  • d ‚Üí distance moved (m)

Conditions:

  • If force ‚üÇ motion ‚Üí W = 0
  • If no displacement ‚Üí W = 0
  • If object moves opposite force ‚Üí work is negative

Example:
A 60 N force pushes a box 4 m:
W = 60 √ó 4 = 240 J

Real-life Uses: lifting loads, pushing carts, pulling ropes, using tools.

Efficiency

What is Efficiency?

Efficiency measures how much input energy becomes useful output. No machine is 100% efficient because some energy is lost to heat, friction, or sound.

Formula: Œ∑ = (Useful Output / Total Input) √ó 100%

Example:
A machine uses 200 J but outputs only 150 J:
Efficiency = (150 / 200) √ó 100% = 75%

Ways to Improve Efficiency:

  • Reduce friction (using oil or lubrication)
  • Use smoother surfaces
  • Improve motor design
  • Use lightweight materials

Real-life Uses: appliances, electric cars, power plants, lighting systems.

Power

What is Power?

Power is the rate at which work is done or energy is used. Higher power means a task is completed faster.

Formulas for Power:

  • P = F √ó v   (Force √ó velocity)
  • P = F √ó (distance / time)
  • P = Work / time
  • P = Energy / time

Units: Watt (W) where 1 W = 1 Joule/second

Examples:

  • Running up stairs faster means using more power.
  • A 1000 W appliance consumes energy faster than a 300 W one.

Applications: motors, vehicles, workouts, machines, electrical devices.

Real-life Applications of Work, Energy & Power

Everyday Situations:

  • Lifting a backpack: increases PE
  • Running or biking: high KE, depends on speed
  • Climbing stairs: doing work against gravity; higher power if done faster
  • Using an elevator: motor does work quickly ‚Üí high power

Machines:

  • Cars: convert chemical ‚Üí kinetic energy; engine power affects acceleration
  • Hydropower plants: PE of water ‚Üí electrical energy
  • Generators: mechanical ‚Üí electrical energy
  • Bulldozers/cranes: high power output for heavy lifting

Sports:

  • Basketball jump shot: PE at highest point
  • Soccer kick: KE transferred to ball
  • Sprinters: high power output in short bursts

Home Appliances:

  • Washing machines: use power to rotate the drum
  • Air conditioners: consume electrical energy, rated by power (kW)
  • Microwave ovens: high power ‚Üí faster heating
